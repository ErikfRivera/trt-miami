# Does Insurance Cover TRT in Miami?

Most commercial insurance plans and Medicare Part B/D cover testosterone replacement therapy in Miami when there is a documented diagnosis of male hypogonadism (ICD-10 E29.1), confirmed by two morning total testosterone draws below 300 ng/dL per AUA guidelines. Coverage typically includes the medication, baseline and follow-up labs, and physician visits. Strong Health works with Florida Blue, Aetna, Cigna, UnitedHealthcare, Humana, and Medicare. This page explains what insurance plans cover, the prior-authorization process most carriers require, what TRT looks like with versus without insurance, and how to appeal a denial.

## Does insurance cover TRT? — short answer

Most major insurers will cover medically necessary TRT when total testosterone is below the diagnostic threshold (typically <300 ng/dL on two early-morning draws) and clinical symptoms are documented [2][3]. Coverage typically extends to the medication itself, the labs needed to diagnose and monitor therapy, and the clinician visits that go with it. Specifics — copay, deductible, formulary tier, and prior-authorization criteria — vary widely between payers and plans, which is why benefits should be verified before you start. ## What insurance covers (and doesn't) for TRT

Coverage scope is generally consistent across major payers, but the line between "covered" and "patient responsibility" depends on the diagnosis, formulary, and plan design.

### Medication

Injectable testosterone cypionate is the most commonly covered formulation because it is inexpensive, generic, and well-established for treating hypogonadism. Branded topical gels (such as AndroGel, Testim, Fortesta, Vogelxo, and Natesto) are often covered on higher formulary tiers, sometimes after step therapy through a generic injection. Compounded creams and compounded pellets are usually treated as non-formulary and are most often billed cash-pay even when an insurance plan covers other testosterone products.

### Labs (baseline, follow-up)

Diagnostic labs — total testosterone, free testosterone, SHBG, estradiol, LH/FSH, CBC, CMP, lipid panel, and PSA in age-appropriate patients — are commonly covered when ordered against a hypogonadism diagnosis or a documented symptom code. Follow-up labs at steady state and the annual monitoring panel that AUA and Endocrine Society guidelines recommend are typically covered on the same basis [2][3].

### Clinician visits

Office visits with your prescribing clinician — the initial consult, dose titration visits, and ongoing monitoring visits — are generally covered as standard office E/M services. Telehealth follow-ups are widely covered in Florida under current rules, though some plans still require an in-person visit at intake.

### What's usually excluded

Insurance plans typically do not cover TRT prescribed for:

- "Low T" framed as a lifestyle or wellness complaint without a confirmed hypogonadism diagnosis.
- Anti-aging, athletic performance, or body-composition goals.
- Branded or compounded products when a covered generic equivalent is available and step therapy has not been completed.
- Pellet insertion procedures under some plans, even when the underlying diagnosis is covered.

TRT is FDA-approved for men with diagnosed hypogonadism — not for low testosterone presented as a lifestyle issue — and payer policies follow that distinction closely.

## How to qualify for TRT coverage

Qualifying for coverage means building a chart that any payer's medical reviewer can sign off on. The criteria are well-aligned across the major guidelines.

### The hypogonadism diagnosis

Coverage requires a documented diagnosis of male hypogonadism using the hypogonadism diagnosis code your clinician uses to bill (testicular hypofunction or, where appropriate, a pituitary-origin code) [1]. The diagnosis must be supported by symptoms and labs in the chart, not just listed on the claim.

### Two morning total T draws <300 ng/dL

The AUA Guideline on Testosterone Deficiency and the Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline both require two separate early-morning (typically 7–10 AM) total testosterone measurements below 300 ng/dL before TRT is initiated [2][3]. A single low reading, or a reading drawn in the afternoon, is the single most common reason payers reject prior authorization.

### Symptom documentation

The chart needs to show symptoms consistent with testosterone deficiency — low libido, erectile dysfunction, fatigue, depressed mood, loss of muscle mass, reduced exercise capacity — and the absence of obvious reversible causes (acute illness, opioid use, recent weight change). Carrier medical policies require both biochemical and clinical criteria; either alone is generally not sufficient [4].

### Florida Medicaid

Florida Medicaid coverage for TRT is more restrictive and is generally limited to clearly documented organic hypogonadism. Prior authorization is required and step therapy is enforced.

## Prior authorization explained

Prior authorization is the gate between a written prescription and a filled, covered medication. For testosterone, almost every major payer requires one.

### What carriers require

Most TRT prior authorizations require documented total testosterone <300 ng/dL on two early-morning draws plus symptoms of hypogonadism. Many also require:

- A baseline PSA (for men age 40+ or with elevated baseline risk).
- A baseline hematocrit.
- Documentation that secondary causes have been considered.
- For branded or higher-tier products, evidence that a generic injection was tried or is contraindicated (step therapy).

### How prior auth is handled

In general, prior auths are submitted at the time the prescription is written so the clock starts immediately. Approval timelines run 1–14 business days depending on carrier and plan. If a peer-to-peer review is needed, your prescribing clinician handles that directly with the payer's medical reviewer.

## If your TRT claim is denied

Denials are common and usually fixable. The first step is reading the denial letter carefully — the reason code tells you exactly what to address.

### Common denial reasons

- Total testosterone above the plan's threshold (often 300 or 350 ng/dL).
- Only one lab draw documented, or labs drawn outside the 7–10 AM window.
- Symptom documentation is thin or non-specific.
- Request is for a non-formulary brand when a covered generic exists.
- Plan reads the request as off-label (wellness, anti-aging, athletic).
- Missing baseline PSA or hematocrit in an age-appropriate patient.

### How to appeal

A typical appeal pathway has three steps: an internal first-level appeal with corrected documentation, an internal second-level appeal if the first is denied, and an external independent review. Most appeals are won at the first level when the underlying issue is documentation — a second AM draw, a fuller symptom list, or evidence of step therapy. Where a peer-to-peer is offered, take it; the approval rate is materially higher than written-only appeals. Your prescribing clinician should be willing to write the appeal letter and join the peer-to-peer call.

## FAQ

### Does Medicare cover TRT in Miami?

Yes — Medicare Part B covers physician visits and labs for diagnosed hypogonadism, and Medicare Part D / Medicare Advantage typically covers testosterone medication after diagnosis. Coverage requires hypogonadism diagnosis-code documentation and AUA-aligned diagnostic labs [2][5].

### Does Florida Blue (BCBS) cover testosterone replacement therapy?

Florida Blue plans typically cover TRT for documented hypogonadism, with prior authorization for medication. Coverage and copay vary by plan tier and formulary status of the prescribed product. Carrier medical policy is the source of truth on a given plan year [4].

### Why does insurance deny TRT?

Common denial reasons: total testosterone above plan threshold, only one lab draw, lab drawn outside the AM window, insufficient symptom documentation, request for off-label use, request for a non-formulary brand when a generic exists.

### Do I need a referral to start TRT under insurance?

Most HMO plans require a PCP referral; PPO plans typically don't. We verify referral requirements during intake.

### How long does prior authorization take?

Typically 1–14 business days depending on carrier. The PA is generally submitted at the consult so therapy can start as soon as approval lands.

### Is self-pay cheaper than using insurance for TRT?

For uninsured patients or for those with high-deductible plans, a transparent cash-pay program is often the lower out-of-pocket option. We'll run both numbers during your consult.
