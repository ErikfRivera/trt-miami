# Testosterone Gel TRT in Miami

Topical testosterone gels — AndroGel, Testim, Fortesta, and Vogelxo — are FDA-approved transdermal TRT options that deliver testosterone through the skin once daily. At Strong Health Miami, gels are typically the choice for patients who want a needle-free, easily reversible protocol, who want to titrate slowly, or who are evaluating TRT for the first time. This page explains how testosterone gels work, the differences between AndroGel 1% and AndroGel 1.62%, application instructions, transfer-safety rules (FDA boxed warning), monitoring labs, side effects, costs, and how gels compare to TRT injections and pellets.

Testosterone replacement therapy is FDA-approved for men with clinically diagnosed hypogonadism — not for general "low energy" or lifestyle complaints. Diagnosis typically requires two early-morning total testosterone draws below the laboratory reference range alongside documented symptoms. The information below is educational; your individual protocol will be determined by your prescribing clinician based on your labs and medical history.

## How testosterone gels work

Topical testosterone gels are alcohol-based hydroalcoholic preparations that deposit testosterone into the stratum corneum, which then acts as a slow-release reservoir into the systemic circulation [1]. After daily application, most patients reach a stable serum concentration within roughly 14 days, and steady-state pharmacokinetics support the once-daily dosing schedule used across every FDA-approved branded gel [2].

### Transdermal absorption and steady-state pharmacokinetics

Because absorption occurs continuously through the skin, gels produce a flatter serum curve than weekly intramuscular injections. There is no post-injection peak and no end-of-week trough. The trade-off is variability — skin thickness, hydration, swimming, and the timing of showers can each shift absorption, which is why daily consistency and follow-up labs at steady state matter [3]. Standard practice is to recheck serum testosterone at trough (just before the next morning's dose) approximately two to four weeks after initiation, then again at three to six months.

### AndroGel 1% vs 1.62% — dose differences

AndroGel 1% delivers 50 mg of testosterone per 5 g packet and is applied to a relatively larger surface area (shoulders, upper arms, and abdomen) [1]. AndroGel 1.62% is a more concentrated formulation that delivers 20.25 mg per pump actuation; a standard starting dose is 40.5 mg (two actuations) daily, applied only to the shoulders and upper arms. The 1.62% formulation has largely supplanted the 1% in current practice because the smaller application area is easier to cover with a t-shirt and somewhat reduces the surface available for secondary transfer. Industry-standard topical dose ranges typically span 20.25–81 mg/day for AndroGel 1.62%, titrated to a serum total testosterone in the mid-normal range of approximately 350–750 ng/dL [5].

## Available gels and topical options

Strong Health Miami clinicians evaluate the full range of FDA-approved transdermal testosterone products. Common topical testosterone options include AndroGel® 1.62%, Testim®, Fortesta®, Vogelxo®, Natesto® (nasal gel), and compounded testosterone cream (5–10% strength). Your clinician will select the right formulation based on your labs, lifestyle, and insurance coverage.

### AndroGel

The most widely prescribed branded transdermal testosterone in the United States. Available as 1% (50 mg/5 g packet, applied to shoulders, upper arms, or abdomen) and 1.62% (20.25 mg per pump, applied to shoulders and upper arms) [1].

### Testim

A 1% gel delivering 50 mg testosterone per 5 g tube. Industry dosing typically starts at 50 mg/day and may be titrated up to 100 mg/day. Some patients find Testim's scent and absorption profile preferable to AndroGel.

### Fortesta

A 2% metered-dose gel applied to the inner thighs rather than the upper body. Typical starting dose is 40 mg/day with a range of 10–70 mg/day. The thigh application site is useful for patients whose work or family routines make upper-body application impractical.

### Vogelxo

A 1% gel positioned as a lower-cost AndroGel 1% equivalent. Typical starting dose is 50 mg/day with titration up to 100 mg/day.

### Natesto (nasal gel)

An adjacent option worth mentioning: Natesto is a nasal testosterone gel dosed three times daily (11 mg per nostril, 33 mg/day total). Because it is not applied to the skin, Natesto largely sidesteps the transfer-safety concerns covered below — though the three-times-daily schedule is a meaningful adherence trade-off.

### Compounded testosterone cream

A 503A-pharmacy compounded cream (commonly 5–10% strength) can be considered when branded gels are cost-prohibitive or when a clinician determines a different vehicle is appropriate. Compounded products are not FDA-approved for the indication, and quality varies between pharmacies — your prescribing clinician will discuss the trade-offs.

## How to apply testosterone gel correctly

Application technique materially affects both the dose absorbed and the risk of transfer to others.

### Where to apply (per FDA label)

Every gel has a label-specified application site, and these are not interchangeable. AndroGel 1% may be applied to the shoulders, upper arms, or abdomen; AndroGel 1.62% is restricted to shoulders and upper arms only [1]. Fortesta is applied only to the inner thighs. Testim and Vogelxo are applied to the shoulders and upper arms. The site is chosen to standardize absorption and to make the area easy to cover with a shirt.

### Drying time, the shirt rule, and hand-washing

The general protocol across products is: apply once daily in the morning to clean, dry, intact skin; allow the gel to dry for the time specified on the product label (typically several minutes); wash your hands with soap and water immediately after application; and cover the application site with clothing once the gel has dried.

### Bathing and swimming guidance

Allow several hours between application and showering, swimming, or strenuous sweating to ensure adequate absorption. If circumstances require early bathing, discuss timing adjustments with your prescribing clinician — do not double-dose to compensate.

## FDA boxed warning: secondary skin transfer

Testosterone gels carry an FDA boxed warning — the agency's strongest labeling — because of documented cases of secondary exposure in women and children who came into skin-to-skin contact with men using the gels [1].

### What the boxed warning says

The FDA boxed warning instructs patients to wash their hands thoroughly with soap and water after every application, to cover the application site with clothing once the gel has dried, and to avoid skin-to-skin contact at the application site with women and children until the area has been washed [1]. Secondary exposure has been associated with virilization in children — including premature puberty signs, accelerated bone age, and behavioral changes — and with virilization symptoms in adult women. If unintentional contact occurs, the contacted skin should be washed with soap and water as soon as possible.

### Daily-life rules to prevent secondary exposure

Practical translation of the warning: apply the gel after your morning shower and before getting dressed; wash your hands at the sink before touching anyone; wear a t-shirt or long-sleeve shirt covering the application area until you have showered that evening; and avoid picking up small children, sharing a bed without clothing, or close cuddling on the application site until showered. These are not optional considerations — they are the basis for the regulatory approval of the entire class.

## Who is a candidate for testosterone gel TRT

Topical gels are well-suited to men with confirmed hypogonadism who prefer a needle-free protocol, who want a slower and more easily reversible titration than weekly injections offer, or who are evaluating TRT for the first time. Gels are generally less appropriate for men who live in close skin-to-skin contact with young children or pregnant partners, for men whose work involves frequent showering or full-body sweat exposure shortly after application, or for those whose serum testosterone fails to reach the target range on a maximum-tolerated topical dose. Candidate selection is always made by your prescribing clinician after review of labs, symptoms, and household considerations.

## Monitoring and labs

A comprehensive baseline panel typically includes total testosterone, free testosterone, SHBG, estradiol (E2), hematocrit, PSA, LH and FSH, CBC, CMP, and a lipid panel — what SERP-leading cash-pay clinics routinely order as a comprehensive workup. After initiating a topical gel, follow-up labs are commonly drawn two to four weeks in for an early read on absorption, then again at three to six months and annually thereafter, consistent with Endocrine Society guidance [5].

Two safety thresholds drive most dose adjustments. Hematocrit is monitored on every panel; AUA guidance suggests investigation at hematocrit greater than 50% and dose reduction or temporary discontinuation at hematocrit 54% or higher. PSA is rechecked at three to twelve months on therapy; urology referral is warranted if PSA rises more than 1.4 ng/mL within a year or exceeds 4.0 ng/mL.

## Side effects and risks

Class-wide androgen effects can include acne, oily skin, accelerated male-pattern hair loss in genetically susceptible men, mood changes, increased red blood cell mass (erythrocytosis), and elevated estradiol [3] [4]. Gel-specific issues include local skin reactions at the application site — redness, itching, dryness — and the absorption variability noted above. Long-term studies of AndroGel in hypogonadal men have demonstrated efficacy with adverse-effect rates broadly consistent with the class, though individual response varies [2]. ## Testosterone gels vs injections vs pellets

| Modality | Frequency | Speed to therapeutic range | Reversibility | Transfer risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Topical gel | Daily | ~2–4 weeks | High (stop daily dose) | Yes — FDA boxed warning |
| [Injection](/trt-injections/) | 1–2× weekly | Days | Moderate | None |
| [Pellet](/trt-pellets/) | Every 3–6 months | Weeks | Low (implanted) | None |

Gels offer the most stable daily curve and the easiest exit; injections offer the fastest titration and lowest monthly cost for most patients; pellets offer the lowest day-to-day maintenance. Choice is individual and should be revisited at each follow-up.

## FAQ

**Is testosterone gel as effective as injections?**
For many patients with mild-to-moderate hypogonadism, gels reach therapeutic total testosterone within 2–4 weeks. Injections raise levels faster and offer easier dose adjustment. Choice depends on lifestyle, lab response, and skin absorption.

**Can I transfer testosterone gel to my partner or kids?**
Yes — the FDA placed a boxed warning on testosterone gels because of documented secondary transfer. Wash hands after application, cover the site with clothing once dry, and avoid skin-to-skin contact at the application site until showered.

**How long does testosterone gel take to work?**
Total testosterone reaches steady-state in roughly 14 days of daily use. Symptom improvement (energy, libido) typically appears between weeks 2 and 6.

**AndroGel 1% vs 1.62% — what's the difference?**
AndroGel 1% is 50 mg/5 g packet; 1.62% delivers 40.5 mg per 2-pump actuation and is applied to a smaller area (upper arms/shoulders). 1.62% is more concentrated and is the more commonly prescribed formulation today.

**Do testosterone gels cause hair loss or acne?**
Both are possible class effects, not gel-specific. They reflect raised androgen levels and are typically managed by dose adjustment or DHT-modifying co-therapy if clinically appropriate.
