# TRT Side Effects: Common, Notable, and Rare

Most men on testosterone replacement therapy tolerate it well, but TRT has a defined set of side effects that fall into three buckets. Common effects are mild and dose-related — acne, increased red blood cell count, and water retention. Notable effects require active monitoring — gynecomastia, erythrocytosis (high hematocrit), fertility suppression, and sleep-apnea worsening. Rare effects are uncommon but documented in the peer-reviewed literature. This page lists every side effect Mayo Clinic, Cleveland Clinic, Harvard Men's Health, and the NIH have catalogued, organized by modality (injections, pellets, gels), with how Strong Health Miami monitors and manages each one.

This is general educational information, not medical advice. TRT is FDA-approved for men with a clinical diagnosis of hypogonadism, and treatment decisions should be made with a prescribing clinician who has reviewed your labs, symptoms, and history. For a broader risk-benefit discussion, see [is TRT safe?](/is-trt-safe/).

## Most common TRT side effects

These are the effects men most often notice in the first 8–12 weeks. They are typically dose-related, easing when dose is reduced, the injection interval is adjusted, or the modality is changed. The same short list shows up across Mayo Clinic [4], Cleveland Clinic [5], the NIH risk review [2], and the Endocrine Society guideline [7].

### Erythrocytosis / polycythemia (raised hematocrit)

An increase in red blood cell mass is the most common dose-limiting side effect of TRT. Testosterone stimulates erythropoiesis, which can push hematocrit above the normal range and, at higher levels, raise concerns about blood viscosity [2][7]. The Endocrine Society guideline recommends withholding testosterone when hematocrit exceeds 54% and resuming at a lower dose once it normalizes; the AUA frames the same threshold and considers therapeutic phlebotomy acceptable [7].

In Miami clinical practice, hematocrit is checked at baseline, around 6 weeks into therapy, at 3 months, and every 6 to 12 months thereafter. Dose reduction, longer injection intervals, or a modality switch are common responses when hematocrit drifts upward.

### Acne and oily skin

Mild acne and increased skin oiliness are common, especially during the first 2–3 months of treatment, and usually settle as serum levels stabilize [4][5]. Standard dermatologic care — gentle cleansing, topical retinoids or benzoyl peroxide where appropriate — is generally enough. Worsening cystic acne may prompt a dose review.

### Water retention and mild weight gain

Some men notice mild fluid retention, especially early in titration. This is typically modest and resolves with dose adjustment [4]. Men with heart failure, advanced kidney disease, or uncontrolled hypertension warrant extra caution and closer follow-up [2][5].

### Injection-site irritation (injection modality)

For IM or subcutaneous testosterone cypionate, transient soreness, redness, or a small lump at the injection site is common and self-limited. Rotating sites, warming the vial, and slow injection generally help. Infrequent infections or sterile abscesses are documented and need prompt evaluation.

### Skin reaction at application site (gel modality)

Topical gels can produce mild redness, itching, or dryness at the application site [4]. Rotating sites per product label and washing hands after application reduce both local reactions and secondary transfer (discussed below).

## Notable TRT side effects (require monitoring)

These are less universal but clinically important — usually the reason a protocol is changed, not stopped.

### Gynecomastia and estradiol shifts

A portion of administered testosterone aromatizes to estradiol. Higher estradiol can drive breast tenderness and, in some men, gynecomastia (glandular breast tissue) [1][2]. Baseline and on-therapy estradiol testing is standard. Management ranges from dose reduction and slower titration to clinician-directed aromatase inhibitor use in selected cases — individualized, not a default add-on.

### Fertility suppression and testicular atrophy

Exogenous testosterone suppresses the HPG axis, lowers LH and FSH, and reduces intratesticular testosterone, in turn reducing sperm production and testicular volume [2][7]. For men who may want children, standard TRT alone is not fertility-preserving. Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) co-therapy is commonly used to maintain testicular function in those who need it, and family-planning intent should be raised at the consult — a counseling step highlighted by both Cleveland Clinic and the NIH risk review [2][5].

### Sleep apnea exacerbation

TRT may worsen obstructive sleep apnea in susceptible men [4][5]. Screening for snoring, witnessed apneas, and daytime sleepiness at intake is standard, and any new or worsening symptoms during therapy warrant a sleep-medicine referral.

### Mood changes (peak/trough on injections)

Less-frequent, larger-dose schedules (e.g., 200 mg every 2 weeks) can produce noticeable peak/trough swings in energy, libido, and mood [5][7]. Weekly or twice-weekly dosing typically smooths these without changing the total weekly dose.

### Scalp hair loss (DHT-driven)

Testosterone converts to dihydrotestosterone (DHT), the active androgen at the scalp follicle. In men genetically predisposed to male-pattern hair loss, TRT can accelerate that process [2][6]. A 5-alpha-reductase inhibitor may be considered by the prescribing clinician, but has its own side-effect profile.

## Rare but documented side effects

These are uncommon but real, appear in the peer-reviewed risk literature, and warrant the monitoring described below.

### Venous thromboembolism (VTE)

DVT and pulmonary embolism have been described in association with TRT, particularly alongside erythrocytosis or underlying thrombophilia [2]. The TRAVERSE trial reported a slightly higher incidence of pulmonary embolism in the testosterone group versus placebo, even though it did not show an increase in major adverse cardiovascular events overall [3]. Men with a prior clotting history, known thrombophilia, or significant immobility need an explicit risk discussion before starting.

### Worsening urinary symptoms / BPH

Some men with pre-existing benign prostatic hyperplasia notice worsening lower-urinary-tract symptoms on TRT [2][5]. Baseline symptom review and on-therapy reassessment are standard.

### PSA elevation

TRT can cause modest PSA increases. The Endocrine Society advises baseline PSA in men 40+ with PSA above ~0.6 ng/mL, a follow-up PSA 3–12 months after starting, and urology referral if PSA rises more than 1.4 ng/mL in 12 months or exceeds 4.0 ng/mL [7]. TRT is generally not initiated when baseline PSA exceeds 4 (or 3 in higher-risk men). Observed PSA shifts may also reflect detection bias rather than new cancer biology [2].

### Skin transfer (gels — FDA boxed warning)

All FDA-approved testosterone gels carry a boxed warning describing the risk of secondary transfer to women and children through skin-to-skin contact, with reported cases of virilization in exposed children. Patients should wash hands thoroughly after application, cover the site with clothing once dry, and avoid skin contact with others — particularly partners, children, and pregnant women — until the site has been washed [4]. This warning applies to AndroGel® 1.62%, Testim®, Fortesta®, Vogelxo®, and related products; the nasal gel Natesto® has a different exposure profile.

## Side effects by TRT modality

Side-effect frequencies are broadly similar across modalities, but the *pattern* differs. The summary below mirrors how Mayo, Cleveland Clinic, and the NIH discuss the trade-offs [2][4][5].

### Injections

[Testosterone injections](/trt-injections/) (IM or subcutaneous cypionate) are the most common modality in cash-pay programs. Typical dosing is 100–200 mg/week, often split into two doses for steadier levels [7]. Trade-offs: injection-site soreness, occasional bruising, and — on less frequent schedules — peak/trough swings. Hematocrit changes appear across all modalities but most often where weekly serum levels run highest.

### Pellets

[Pellet therapy](/trt-pellets/) (Testopel® or compounded pellets from FDA-registered 503B facilities) provides steady release over 3–6 months. The trade-off: dose cannot be reduced once implanted, so a sharp hematocrit rise leaves phlebotomy as the main option. Local complications include extrusion and infection at the insertion site.

### Gels

[Testosterone gels](/trt-gels/) avoid needles and produce smooth daily levels, but absorption varies between men, application-site reactions are common, and the FDA-boxed secondary-transfer warning applies [4]. Common options include AndroGel® 1.62%, Testim®, Fortesta®, Vogelxo®, Natesto® (nasal), and compounded testosterone cream.

## How Strong Health Miami monitors and manages side effects

Side-effect management is, in practice, a monitoring program. The pattern Strong Health Miami follows mirrors industry-standard practice and Endocrine Society guidance [7]:

- **Comprehensive baseline workup.** Total testosterone (on two early-morning draws), free testosterone, SHBG, estradiol, hematocrit, CBC, CMP, lipid panel, PSA (in men 40+), and LH/FSH.
- **Symptom screening.** Sleep-apnea symptoms, urinary symptoms, family-planning intent, breast tenderness, mood, and acne.
- **Recheck cadence.** A follow-up around 6 weeks into therapy during titration, again at 3 months, and then every 6 to 12 months once stable — consistent with both AUA and Endocrine Society guidance [7].
- **Therapeutic target.** Mid-normal serum testosterone, generally in the 400–700 ng/dL range per the Endocrine Society and roughly 450–600 ng/dL per the AUA, measured at trough for injections [7].
- **Hematocrit threshold.** Investigate at hematocrit above 50%; reduce dose or hold and consider therapeutic phlebotomy at hematocrit at or above 54% [7].
- **PSA threshold.** Urology referral if PSA rises more than 1.4 ng/mL in 12 months or exceeds 4.0 ng/mL [7].

All prescribing and dose decisions are made under physician supervision by your prescribing clinician; the framework above is general and is individualized at consult. ## When side effects mean we change protocol

Most side effects are managed by dose, interval, or modality changes — not by stopping therapy. Common triggers include:

- Hematocrit trending toward 54% — dose down, lengthen interval, or refer for phlebotomy [7].
- New or worsening sleep apnea symptoms — referral and re-evaluation [4].
- Sustained estradiol-driven gynecomastia or breast tenderness — slow titration or clinician-directed aromatase management [1].
- New family-planning intent — switch to a fertility-preserving regimen with hCG co-therapy or alternative [2].
- PSA shift exceeding the thresholds above — urology referral before continuing [7].
- Peak/trough mood swings on q2-week dosing — switch to weekly or twice-weekly.

## TRT side effects vs natural aging — telling them apart

Many "side effects" patients worry about — fatigue, weight gain, low libido, mood changes — are also symptoms of untreated low testosterone or aging [4][6]. A few distinguishing markers:

- **Dose-related effects** (acne, hematocrit, water retention) appear *after* starting TRT and track with serum levels.
- **Aging-related complaints** are slow-onset, predate therapy, and do not respond to dose adjustment.
- **Lab-based effects** (hematocrit, PSA, estradiol) are objective and only detectable with the monitoring panel.

This is why baseline labs and a documented symptom diary matter — they separate "this is the medication" from "this is life."

## Long-term TRT side effects — what the evidence shows

Long-term safety data has improved substantially in the past decade. The TRAVERSE trial (NEJM 2023) randomized middle-aged and older men with hypogonadism and cardiovascular risk to testosterone gel versus placebo and found testosterone non-inferior for major adverse cardiovascular events; a small absolute increase in pulmonary embolism, atrial fibrillation, and acute kidney injury was reported in the testosterone arm [3]. The NIH risk review catalogues the same long-term considerations — erythrocytosis, sleep-apnea worsening, prostate monitoring, and fertility suppression — as the main signals warranting follow-up [2]. Mayo, Cleveland Clinic, and Harvard frame the long-term picture similarly: managed monitoring, not avoidance [4][5][6]. For men in Miami-Dade weighing the decision, the practical implication is that long-term TRT is reasonable when paired with structured monitoring and a clinician willing to change course when the labs say so. ## FAQ

**What is the most common side effect of TRT?**
Erythrocytosis — an elevated red blood cell count / hematocrit — is the most common dose-limiting side effect of TRT. Standard practice, including at Strong Health Miami, is to monitor hematocrit at week 6, month 3, and biannually thereafter, and to reduce dose or refer for therapeutic phlebotomy if hematocrit approaches 54%, in line with Endocrine Society and AUA guidance [7].

**Does TRT cause hair loss?**
TRT can accelerate male-pattern hair loss in men who are genetically predisposed, because testosterone converts to DHT (the active androgen at the scalp follicle) [2][6]. Co-therapy with a 5-alpha-reductase inhibitor is sometimes used but has its own side-effect profile and is a clinician-led decision.

**Will TRT shrink my testicles?**
TRT suppresses the HPG axis, which reduces endogenous testosterone production and can reduce testicular volume [2]. HCG (human chorionic gonadotropin) co-therapy maintains testicular function and fertility for men who need it.

**Does TRT cause acne?**
Mild acne is a common, dose-related effect of TRT, especially during the first 2–3 months [4][5]. It usually settles as dose stabilizes; standard topical care is generally sufficient.

**Will TRT make me infertile?**
TRT suppresses sperm production for most men [2][7]. Men who want to preserve fertility should not start standard TRT alone — HCG co-therapy or alternative protocols are required. Discuss family-planning intent at the consult.

**Are TRT side effects worse with injections, pellets, or gels?**
Each modality has its own profile. Injections produce peak/trough mood and energy swings on less-frequent schedules; pellets are steadier but offer no dose-down once implanted; gels carry the FDA-boxed secondary-transfer warning [4]. Monitoring is similar across all three [7].

**Do TRT side effects go away when you stop?**
Most dose-related effects (acne, hematocrit elevation, water retention) resolve over weeks once therapy is discontinued. Testicular volume usually recovers, but fertility recovery varies and may take 6–18 months [2].
