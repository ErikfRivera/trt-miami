# TRT Injections in Miami

Testosterone injections are the most-prescribed form of TRT in the United States, and standard injectable protocols use weekly intramuscular cypionate, biweekly enanthate, or subcutaneous (subQ) administration under physician supervision. Injectable testosterone replacement therapy raises serum total and free testosterone faster than gels or pellets and supports titration to a steady physiologic range using periodic labs (total T, free T, SHBG, estradiol, hematocrit). This page explains the injectable options, who they're appropriate for under Endocrine Society and AUA guidelines, what monitoring looks like, and how much TRT injections typically cost in Miami.

TRT is a prescription therapy approved by the FDA for men with a clinical diagnosis of hypogonadism — not for general "low T" lifestyle complaints [1][4].

## How testosterone injections work

Injectable testosterone is bioidentical hormone dissolved in an oil carrier and esterified — a fatty-acid chain is attached so the body releases it slowly over days. After injection, the body cleaves the ester and free testosterone enters circulation, raising serum total and free T into a therapeutic window typically defined as the mid-normal range, around 400–700 ng/dL per Endocrine Society guidance, or 450–600 ng/dL per the AUA, measured midway between injections [1][2].

### Esters explained — cypionate, enanthate, propionate, undecanoate (Aveed)

- **Testosterone cypionate (Depo-Testosterone).** Most widely used ester in U.S. TRT. Half-life ~8 days. Weekly or twice-weekly dosing.
- **Testosterone enanthate.** Similar half-life and clinical profile to cypionate. Often dosed biweekly, though many clinicians favor weekly for steadier kinetics.
- **Testosterone propionate.** Short half-life (~2 days). Rarely used for routine TRT.
- **Testosterone undecanoate (Aveed).** Long-acting deep IM injection every 10 weeks after a loading dose. Carries an FDA-mandated in-office observation period for a rare risk of pulmonary oil microembolism [4].
- **Xyosted.** FDA-approved once-weekly subQ auto-injector of testosterone enanthate for at-home use.

### Intramuscular vs subcutaneous (subQ) delivery and injection sites

Intramuscular (IM) injections deposit testosterone into muscle — typically the gluteal, vastus lateralis (outer thigh), or deltoid. Subcutaneous (subQ) injections go into the fat layer of the abdomen or thigh using a shorter, thinner needle. Peer-reviewed data show subQ cypionate produces comparable trough levels and symptom response to IM, with less injection-site pain and steadier kinetics. SubQ is the default at many cash-pay TRT clinics for needle-averse patients.

### Half-life and dose cadence — weekly, biweekly, monthly

The ester's half-life drives cadence. Cypionate and enanthate, with roughly week-long half-lives, support weekly or twice-weekly dosing for the steadiest serum curve. The legacy FDA label permits every-2-to-4-week IM dosing of 50–400 mg, but current Endocrine Society practice patterns favor weekly or twice-weekly to avoid peak-and-trough swings in mood, libido, and hematocrit [2]. Aveed is dosed every 10 weeks. See also [testosterone pellets](/trt-pellets/) and [testosterone gels](/trt-gels/).

## Who is a candidate for TRT injections in Miami

### Symptomatic hypogonadism (low testosterone) — primary vs secondary

TRT is indicated for men with **symptomatic hypogonadism** — both biochemical evidence of low testosterone and clinical symptoms such as low libido, erectile dysfunction, fatigue, loss of morning erections, decreased muscle mass, depressed mood, or decreased bone density [1][2]. Primary hypogonadism originates in the testes; secondary in the pituitary or hypothalamus (the HPG axis). Secondary hypogonadism may warrant additional pituitary evaluation before therapy.

### Two-morning total testosterone diagnosis per AUA Guideline

The AUA and Endocrine Society both require **two separate early-morning total testosterone measurements** before initiating TRT, because levels fluctuate diurnally and a single low draw is not diagnostic [1][2]. Most TRT clinics use a threshold of total testosterone below 300 ng/dL on two AM draws, paired with documented symptoms. Free testosterone and SHBG are added when total T is borderline.

### Contraindications and shared-decision factors

TRT is generally not initiated in men with active prostate or breast cancer, untreated severe sleep apnea, severe untreated lower urinary tract symptoms, uncontrolled heart failure, a recent cardiovascular event, elevated baseline hematocrit, or PSA above 4 ng/mL (above 3 in higher-risk men) until urology clears the patient [1][4]. Men actively trying to conceive should discuss alternatives, because exogenous testosterone suppresses spermatogenesis.

## Injection protocols we use at Strong Health

Specific dose, ester, route, and cadence are always set by your prescribing clinician based on your labs and symptom response. The ranges below describe what's typical at U.S. cash-pay TRT clinics and is published in the Endocrine Society and AUA guidelines [1][2] — not a fixed Strong Health protocol.

### Standard weekly cypionate (typical 100–200 mg/week range — clinician-set)

A typical starting dose for testosterone cypionate is **50–100 mg/week**, given IM or subQ, with maintenance commonly in the **100–200 mg/week** range (most men settle around 120–160 mg/week). The Mayo Clinic monograph cites 75 mg subQ weekly as a common starting point [5]. The legacy FDA label allows 50–400 mg every 2–4 weeks IM, but most current TRT practices have moved to weekly or twice-weekly dosing for steadier serum levels.

### SubQ alternative (Xyosted auto-injector)

For patients who prefer not to draw their own injections, the FDA-approved Xyosted auto-injector delivers testosterone enanthate subcutaneously once weekly at fixed doses (50, 75, or 100 mg). It carries an FDA-required warning about possible blood pressure increases, which is why we monitor blood pressure at every visit [4].

### In-clinic vs supervised at-home self-administration

Some Miami patients prefer to come in weekly for a nurse-administered injection; others — particularly on twice-weekly subQ — prefer self-administration at home after a teaching visit. Both are acceptable when paired with appropriate lab monitoring.

## Monitoring and labs

### Required baseline panel (total T, free T, SHBG, estradiol, hematocrit, PSA, CMP, lipid)

Before initiating injectable TRT, most cash-pay TRT clinics order a comprehensive baseline panel: total and free testosterone, SHBG, estradiol (E2), hematocrit/CBC, PSA (in men ≥40 or per Endocrine Society criteria), LH and FSH (to differentiate primary vs secondary), comprehensive metabolic panel, and a lipid panel [1][2]. A baseline PSA plus digital rectal exam is recommended in men 40 and over with PSA above 0.6 ng/mL.

### Follow-up cadence (week 6, month 3, month 6, then biannual)

The Endocrine Society recommends a follow-up at **3–6 months** after starting therapy, then annually [2]. The AUA recommends a follow-up testosterone level at steady state, then every 6–12 months [1]. A common cash-pay clinic pattern is a recheck around **week 6** during titration, **quarterly through year one**, then **every 6–12 months** thereafter. Hematocrit, estradiol, and PSA are monitored on the same cadence.

### When we adjust dose

Dose is adjusted by the prescribing clinician based on trough testosterone, symptom response, and safety labs. Hematocrit is the most common driver of dose reduction: most TRT clinics follow AUA and Endocrine Society guidance to **investigate at hematocrit >50%, and reduce the dose, temporarily withhold therapy, or use therapeutic phlebotomy at hematocrit ≥54%** [1][2]. PSA rising more than 1.4 ng/mL in 12 months, or exceeding 4.0 ng/mL, triggers a urology referral [2].

## Side effects and risks of testosterone injections

A fuller discussion lives on our [side effects of TRT](/trt-side-effects/) and [is TRT safe](/is-trt-safe/) pages.

### Common (injection-site irritation, acne, fluid retention)

Most patients tolerate injectable TRT well. The most common effects are mild injection-site soreness or bruising, acne (especially on the back and shoulders during the first few months), mild fluid retention, and oilier skin [5].

### Notable (erythrocytosis/polycythemia, gynecomastia, fertility suppression)

- **Erythrocytosis (polycythemia).** Testosterone stimulates red-blood-cell production; a rise in hematocrit above 54% raises clotting risk and requires dose reduction, drug holiday, or phlebotomy [1][2].
- **Gynecomastia.** Some testosterone aromatizes to estradiol; if E2 climbs disproportionately, breast tenderness or tissue growth can occur and is managed by dose adjustment or, occasionally, an aromatase inhibitor.
- **Fertility suppression.** Exogenous testosterone suppresses the HPG axis (LH/FSH) and reduces sperm production. Men trying to conceive should discuss alternatives such as clomiphene or hCG before starting TRT.
- **Testicular volume reduction.** Suppression of LH leads to a modest decrease in testicular size in many men on TRT; this is reversible if therapy stops.

### Cardiovascular safety — what TRAVERSE (2023) showed

For more than a decade, observational studies raised concern that TRT might increase cardiovascular events, prompting an FDA boxed-warning history on testosterone products [4]. The 2023 **TRAVERSE** randomized trial in the *New England Journal of Medicine* enrolled more than 5,200 middle-aged and older men with hypogonadism and elevated cardiovascular risk and found that testosterone gel **did not increase the incidence of major adverse cardiac events** versus placebo [3]. AUA and Endocrine Society guidelines now support TRT in appropriately diagnosed men, while still requiring individualized cardiovascular risk discussion before initiation [1][2].

## Frequently asked questions

**How often do you get TRT injections?**
Weekly intramuscular or subcutaneous testosterone cypionate is the most common cadence in Miami TRT clinics, with biweekly enanthate and quarterly Aveed available. Frequency is set by your prescribing clinician based on labs, ester half-life, and symptom response, with weekly or twice-weekly increasingly preferred over every-2-to-4-week dosing for steadier serum levels [2].

**Are subcutaneous (subQ) TRT injections as effective as intramuscular?**
Yes. Peer-reviewed data show subQ testosterone cypionate produces comparable trough levels and symptom response to IM, with less injection-site pain and steadier kinetics. SubQ is the default for needle-averse patients at many cash-pay TRT clinics and is the route used by FDA-approved auto-injectors such as Xyosted.

**What is the downside of taking testosterone injections?**
Injections require ongoing labs (hematocrit, estradiol, PSA) and carry risks of polycythemia, acne, fluid retention, gynecomastia, and fertility suppression. None of these are unmanageable when monitored by a board-certified clinician on the cadence recommended by AUA and Endocrine Society guidelines [1][2].

**How many TRT injections per month?**
On a standard weekly cypionate protocol, four injections per month. Biweekly enanthate is two per month. Twice-weekly subQ is eight. Long-acting undecanoate (Aveed) is once every 10 weeks after the loading dose [4].

**Why are some doctors against TRT?**
Older observational studies raised cardiovascular concerns and shaped a cautious FDA boxed-warning history [4]. The 2023 TRAVERSE trial (NEJM) found no increased risk of major cardiac events versus placebo in men with documented hypogonadism, which is why AUA and Endocrine Society guidelines now support TRT in clearly diagnosed patients [1][2][3].

**How small do balls get on TRT?**
Exogenous testosterone suppresses LH, which reduces intratesticular testosterone and can cause a modest decrease in testicular volume. Most men notice a small reduction; the effect is generally reversible if therapy stops. Men trying to preserve fertility or testicular volume can discuss adjuncts such as hCG with their clinician.
