# Is TRT Safe?

Testosterone replacement therapy is safe for men with clinically diagnosed hypogonadism when prescribed and monitored according to AUA and Endocrine Society guidelines. The 2023 TRAVERSE trial — the largest randomized cardiovascular-safety study to date, published in *The New England Journal of Medicine* — found no increased risk of major cardiac events with TRT versus placebo in middle-aged and older men with documented low testosterone. This page summarizes what the trial showed, the safety domains that require monitoring (hematocrit, PSA, estradiol, fertility), who should not start TRT, and how Strong Health Miami's monitoring protocol is built around these guidelines.

## The short answer

For men with biochemically confirmed hypogonadism — that is, low total testosterone documented on two early-morning draws together with clinical symptoms — testosterone replacement therapy is considered safe under current evidence and guidelines, provided the patient is monitored on a defined schedule for hematocrit, PSA, estradiol, and symptoms [1][2][3]. TRT is FDA-approved for this diagnosis. It is not approved as a treatment for non-specific fatigue, "low T" as a lifestyle complaint, or age-related decline in the absence of documented deficiency [3][5].

## What the TRAVERSE trial showed (2023)

The most important modern study on TRT safety is the TRAVERSE trial (Lincoff et al., *New England Journal of Medicine*, 2023) [1]. It is the trial cited by the FDA, the AUA, and most major endocrinology bodies when discussing cardiovascular risk with testosterone replacement.

### Trial design and population

TRAVERSE was a multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled non-inferiority trial. It enrolled approximately 5,200 men aged 45 to 80 with two fasting morning testosterone measurements below the hypogonadal threshold, symptoms of hypogonadism, and either pre-existing cardiovascular disease or a high cardiovascular risk profile [1]. Participants received daily transdermal testosterone gel titrated to mid-normal range or matching placebo, and were followed for a mean of just under two years. The trial was designed to answer the cardiovascular safety question that had hung over the field since the 2014 FDA advisory.

### Cardiovascular outcomes

The primary composite endpoint was major adverse cardiac events (MACE): cardiovascular death, non-fatal myocardial infarction, and non-fatal stroke. TRAVERSE found that testosterone therapy was non-inferior to placebo for this endpoint in men with hypogonadism and elevated cardiovascular risk [1]. In plain language, the trial did not detect an increased risk of heart attack, stroke, or cardiovascular death from TRT versus placebo over the follow-up period. The FDA updated its testosterone product labeling in 2023 based on these results [5].

TRAVERSE did identify higher rates of certain secondary signals in the testosterone group, including atrial fibrillation, acute kidney injury, and pulmonary embolism [1]. These are part of the conversation any prescribing clinician should have before initiating therapy, and they are reasons baseline screening and ongoing monitoring matter.

### What TRAVERSE did not address

TRAVERSE was a cardiovascular safety trial. It was not designed to settle every safety question about TRT. It did not enroll men with active prostate cancer or recent prostate cancer history, it did not enroll men actively trying to conceive, and it did not extend long enough to characterize lifetime risk on therapy [1]. Those domains are addressed by separate evidence — the T-Trials (Snyder et al., *NEJM* 2016) [4], Endocrine Society guidance [2], and AUA guidance [3] — and are discussed below.

## TRT safety by risk domain

Talking about whether TRT is "safe" in the abstract is less useful than walking through the specific organ systems and the monitoring rules that apply to each.

### Cardiovascular

Based on TRAVERSE, current evidence does not support the position that physiologic-range testosterone replacement raises the risk of myocardial infarction, stroke, or cardiovascular death in men with diagnosed hypogonadism [1][5]. Men with a recent major cardiac event — myocardial infarction within the last six months, for example — are generally advised to defer TRT and discuss timing with their cardiologist [3]. Baseline cardiovascular risk assessment, blood pressure, and lipid panel are part of the standard pre-TRT workup [2][3].

### Prostate (PSA monitoring and urology co-management)

The historical concern that TRT causes prostate cancer is not supported by current peer-reviewed evidence in men without active disease [2][3]. What the guidelines do require is structured PSA monitoring. Endocrine Society guidance recommends a baseline PSA in men aged 40 and over, repeat measurement at three to twelve months after starting therapy, and then PSA monitoring on the age-appropriate screening schedule [2]. AUA guidance is aligned [3].

TRT should not be initiated when PSA is above 4 ng/mL, or above 3 ng/mL in patients at higher prostate cancer risk, without urology evaluation first [2]. Urology referral is appropriate if PSA rises by more than 1.4 ng/mL within twelve months or exceeds 4.0 ng/mL on therapy [2]. Active prostate cancer is a contraindication to TRT [2][3]. Men with a personal history of prostate cancer require urology co-management before any decision about replacement therapy.

### Hematologic — polycythemia and erythrocytosis

The most common dose-limiting effect of TRT is erythrocytosis: an increase in red blood cell mass that shows up as a rising hematocrit. It is the single most common reason that prescribing clinicians lower a TRT dose [1][2]. Both major guidelines define explicit thresholds. AUA guidance recommends investigating at a hematocrit above 50% and reducing dose or temporarily holding therapy at a hematocrit of 54% or above [3]. Endocrine Society guidance recommends withholding testosterone at a hematocrit greater than 54% and resuming at a lower dose, with therapeutic phlebotomy as an acceptable adjunct [2].

This is one of the reasons routine bloodwork on TRT is non-negotiable. A patient who feels good on therapy can still develop a hematocrit that needs intervention, and the only way to find it is to draw labs on schedule. For more on the broader effect profile, see [TRT side effects](/trt-side-effects/).

### Fertility — HPG-axis suppression and HCG co-therapy

Exogenous testosterone suppresses the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis. That suppression lowers luteinizing hormone and follicle-stimulating hormone, which in turn lowers intratesticular testosterone and impairs spermatogenesis [2]. The practical consequence is that TRT is not appropriate as a first-line option for men who are actively trying to conceive without a fertility-sparing strategy. Co-therapy with human chorionic gonadotropin (HCG) is one option commonly used to preserve testicular function during TRT; selective estrogen receptor modulators such as clomiphene are another approach used in men with secondary hypogonadism who wish to preserve fertility [2]. The right approach is individual and should be decided with a prescribing clinician before therapy starts.

### Estradiol balance — gynecomastia and mood

Testosterone is aromatized to estradiol. Both hormones are physiologic and necessary in men, but supraphysiologic estradiol can contribute to gynecomastia, water retention, and mood changes [2]. Baseline estradiol is part of a comprehensive baseline panel, and follow-up estradiol is checked when symptoms suggest imbalance. The goal is mid-normal range testosterone with proportional estradiol — not the elimination of estradiol with aromatase inhibitors, which is generally not recommended outside of specific clinical scenarios [2].

### Sleep apnea

Severe untreated obstructive sleep apnea is a recognized cautionary condition for TRT [2][3]. Patients with significant symptoms of sleep apnea — heavy snoring, witnessed apneas, excessive daytime sleepiness — should be evaluated and, where appropriate, treated before initiating testosterone. Patients already on CPAP for diagnosed OSA can generally proceed under physician supervision.

## Who should NOT start TRT

The guidelines are unusually clear about who should not begin testosterone replacement. These are not edge cases — they are firm contraindications or cautions documented in both Endocrine Society [2] and AUA [3] guidance.

### Active prostate cancer

TRT is contraindicated in men with active, untreated prostate cancer [2][3]. Men with a personal history of prostate cancer require urology evaluation and shared decision-making with their oncology team before TRT can be considered.

### Severe untreated obstructive sleep apnea

Severe untreated OSA is a recognized cautionary condition; sleep medicine workup and treatment should precede TRT in symptomatic patients [2][3].

### Hematocrit above 54%

A baseline hematocrit above 54% is a contraindication to starting TRT [2][3]. The same threshold applies on therapy: if hematocrit reaches 54% or higher, dose reduction, temporary discontinuation, or therapeutic phlebotomy is indicated [2][3].

### Trying to conceive (without HCG or alternative strategy)

Men actively trying to father a child should not start standard TRT without a fertility-preserving co-therapy strategy, because exogenous testosterone suppresses spermatogenesis [2]. This is a planning conversation, not a hard refusal — there are options, but they require a deliberate plan.

### Acute myocardial infarction within six months

A recent major cardiovascular event is a reason to defer TRT and revisit timing with cardiology [3]. TRAVERSE excluded patients with a very recent MI from enrollment, and current guidance reflects that caution [1][3].

Other situations that warrant individualized review include uncontrolled severe congestive heart failure, untreated severe lower urinary tract symptoms, elevated baseline PSA pending urology workup, and a personal or strong family history of thrombophilia [2][3].

## How Strong Health Miami monitors TRT safety

At Strong Health Miami, testosterone replacement therapy is provided under Florida-licensed physician supervision, with a monitoring schedule built directly around Endocrine Society [2] and AUA [3] guidance.

### Baseline panel

Before any prescription is written, the standard comprehensive baseline workup for TRT typically includes two early-morning total testosterone draws, free testosterone, sex hormone binding globulin (SHBG), estradiol, hematocrit and full CBC, PSA (for men 40 and over, or earlier if indicated), LH and FSH, a comprehensive metabolic panel, and a lipid panel [2][3]. Cardiovascular risk is assessed at the same visit, including blood pressure and a review of personal and family cardiac history.

### Week 6 / month 3 / month 6 / biannual follow-up

A typical follow-up cadence on TRT is an early follow-up at approximately six weeks during titration, repeat labs at three to six months, and then every six to twelve months once a patient is on a stable regimen [2][3]. Hematocrit and PSA are tracked at every routine follow-up draw. Estradiol is checked when symptoms — for example, nipple tenderness, water retention, or mood change — suggest it should be. Total testosterone is timed to the regimen so the level reflects the patient's typical exposure, not a peak or trough.

### Phlebotomy referral if hematocrit elevates

If hematocrit rises into the cautionary range (above 50%), dose adjustment is discussed [3]. If hematocrit reaches 54% or above, therapy is held or reduced and therapeutic phlebotomy is considered, per guideline [2][3]. This is one of the reasons it is important to be on a program that draws labs on schedule rather than a renewal-only model.

Patients commonly ask which delivery method is "safer." The data does not support large major-organ safety differences between formulations when serum testosterone is kept in the physiologic range and monitoring is performed. Differences across [TRT injections](/trt-injections/), pellets, and gels are largely about pharmacokinetics, convenience, and side effect profile rather than fundamental safety.

## What older safety claims got wrong

A reasonable patient reading older news coverage might conclude that TRT is dangerous. It is worth understanding where that perception came from and why the evidence base has moved.

### The 2014 FDA cardiovascular warning history

In 2014 and 2015, the FDA issued advisories warning about possible cardiovascular risk with testosterone products, driven primarily by two observational studies [5]. Those advisories required a label change and triggered considerable caution in the prescribing community. The studies that drove the warning were observational, not randomized, and had design limitations identified by subsequent analyses.

### Why the field has moved past it

In 2023, the FDA updated its testosterone product labeling following the publication of TRAVERSE, which was the randomized, placebo-controlled evidence that the 2014 advisories had lacked [1][5]. The updated labeling removed the prior cardiovascular risk language for men with hypogonadism and replaced it with language consistent with TRAVERSE findings, while preserving the boxed warnings for secondary transfer in topical products [5]. Major specialty societies — including the AUA in its 2024-reaffirmed guidance [3] and the Endocrine Society in its existing guidance [2] — frame TRT as appropriate for men with documented hypogonadism, under monitoring.

This is not the same thing as saying TRT is risk-free. It is saying that the specific cardiovascular concern that dominated patient conversations a decade ago is now addressed by a high-quality randomized trial whose results favored safety. The monitoring requirements remain unchanged: hematocrit, PSA, estradiol, symptoms, and labs on schedule.

## FAQ

### Is TRT safe long-term?

Yes, when properly monitored. The 2023 TRAVERSE trial (NEJM) followed nearly 5,200 men with hypogonadism for 22 months on average and found no excess major cardiac events vs placebo [1]. Long-term safety is contingent on guideline-aligned monitoring (hematocrit, PSA, estradiol) — see Endocrine Society 2018 [2] and AUA 2024 guidance [3].

### Does TRT cause heart attacks or strokes?

The largest randomized cardiovascular safety trial (TRAVERSE, NEJM 2023) found no increased risk of major adverse cardiac events with TRT versus placebo in men with documented hypogonadism [1]. The FDA updated its 2014 advisory based on this evidence [5].

### Does TRT cause prostate cancer?

Current peer-reviewed evidence does not show that TRT initiates prostate cancer in men without active disease [2][3]. TRT is contraindicated in men with active prostate cancer. PSA is monitored at baseline, 3–6 months, and annually thereafter [2].

### What is the most common side effect of TRT?

Erythrocytosis (raised hematocrit) is the most common dose-limiting effect [1][2]. It is tracked at every follow-up draw; if hematocrit approaches 54%, dose is reduced or the patient is referred for therapeutic phlebotomy [2][3].

### Why do some doctors avoid prescribing TRT?

Earlier observational studies suggested possible cardiovascular harm, and TRT has been over-prescribed historically for non-hypogonadal use [5][6]. The 2023 TRAVERSE results and updated AUA/Endocrine Society guidelines support TRT in clearly diagnosed patients, with careful monitoring [1][2][3].

### Is it safe to be on TRT for life?

For most men with confirmed hypogonadism, TRT is a long-term therapy similar to thyroid hormone replacement [2][7]. Safety hinges on continued monitoring — there is no published evidence of acceptable, sustained off-treatment outcomes in men with primary hypogonadism.
